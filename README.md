**Background:** Adolescence is a critical window for autonomic and limbic development and a period of elevated risk for depression. Although autonomic and limbic system dysregulation have, independently, been linked to depression, they are functionally coupled and both influenced by early life stress (ELS). We do not yet know, however, whether the interaction of autonomic activity and limbic connectivity predicts depressive symptoms and is shaped by dimensions of ELS.

**Methods:** Participants were 93 youth (Mage=12.3) who completed the Trier Social Stress Test (TSST) with continuous electrocardiogram recording. Respiratory heart rate variability (RespHRV) was derived across the baseline, reactivity, and recovery phases of the TSST. Hippocampal-amygdala connectivity was estimated from contemporaneous resting-state data using the Tian atlas. We tested whether RespHRV recovery and connectivity interacted to predict depressive symptoms, and whether ELS dimensions of threat, unpredictability, and deprivation moderated this interaction.

**Results:** Greater RespHRV during recovery was associated with more negative right hippocampal tail-left lateral amygdala connectivity (β=−0.42, q=.002). Threat (β=−0.34, p=.007) and deprivation (β=−0.36, p=.009) independently moderated the interaction of RespHRV recovery and connectivity predicting depressive symptoms. Specifically, more negative connectivity predicted fewer symptoms when paired with weaker RespHRV recovery in high-ELS youth, but with stronger RespHRV recovery in low-ELS youth.

**Conclusions:** Whether autonomic-limbic coupling buffers or confers risk for depression depends on the early environment in which it developed.
